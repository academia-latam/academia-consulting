FIG. 01 · ONE TEACHER, ONE WEEKEND

A high-school teacher in Puebla tried four AI tools over one weekend. Three asked for a credit card the minute the trial ended. The fourth locked her out after ten messages. Monday came anyway: 38 essays to grade, almost no time, and more than half written with AI by her own students, with no way to tell which.

She didn't need a fifth app, or a back-and-forth where one AI grades what another AI wrote. She needed a task built around what her students actually need to learn, one AI can't do for them. That's where we start.

We start with what her students need to learn.

FIG. 04 · WHAT WE BELIEVE

We don't add AI.
We back the teacher.

Most schools buy AI to look modern, and there's still little proof it helps anyone learn. We start somewhere older: the teacher already knows what this room needs. Our job is to make that knowledge go further, and to let a tool in only where it earns its place. AI can write the draft. It can't watch a student go quiet and know why. That's the part we back.
